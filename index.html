Texto completo del artículo
British bartender Jon Travers-Smith has created a cocktail brand called Inigo, which can be served as a non-alcoholic option or mixed with spirits. Inigo debuts with three varieties: Earl Grey Martini, which combines flavours of bergamot, lemon, juniper, and tea; Korean Paloma, featuring notes of grapefruit, lime, gochujang, lapsang souchong smoke, and miso umami; and Bitter Riot, offering a bitter-forward tropical taste with fresh ginger and aromatic spices. All three are designed to be served as standalone non-alcoholic drinks. However, the brand also encourages consumers to consider adding a premium spirit, as part of what it calls the ‘Stick + Twist’ belief. Crafted with a specific spirit in mind, the Earl Grey Martini pairs well with gin, the Korean Paloma with Tequila, and the Bitter Riot with grain vodka. All three varieties are vegan, made with 100% natural ingredients and are free from preservatives, artificial sweeteners, and added colour. Travers-Smith said: “People’s relationship with alcohol has become more nuanced, but the occasion itself has not become any less important. “We wanted to create a drink with the same depth, structure and sense of intent you would expect from a great cocktail, whether or not a spirit is added. With Stick + Twist, the non-alcoholic serve is not a compromise. It is the foundation. The twist is simply the drinker’s choice.” Inigo is now available on the brand’s website with an RRP of £26 (US$35) for a 500ml bottle.

Premium, mid-strength and flavour-first defy beer slowdown

A nivel global, el volumen de cerveza cayó 1% en 2025 en los 21 principales mercados, pero el valor creció gracias a la migración del consumidor hacia marcas premium. Las innovaciones en cervezas sin alcohol y formatos orientados al sabor abren nuevas oportunidades de crecimiento. Este shift hacia premium-and-above se consolida como tendencia estratégica que contrarresta la contracción volumétrica, relevante para portafolios diversificados de empresas argentinas en este segmento.

Leer nota original Ver en español
Texto completo del artículo
New data shows that while global beer volumes may be softening, value growth is holding firm as consumers trade up to premium offerings. Meanwhile, innovations across non-alcoholic beer and demand flavour-led formats are creating new avenues for growth. While global beer volumes declined in 2025 – falling by one per cent in the world’s leading 21 markets – drinks market analyst IWSR has reported slight growth in beer value as consumers lean into premium-and-above brands. IWSR Senior Beer Insight Manager Roisin Vulcheva says: “Beer continues to benefit from consumer willingness to upgrade to more premium offerings – particularly no-alcohol beer, which has seen strong growth in almost all of the top 21 markets, and which skews premium-plus. “The investment by global brewers in premium-plus propositions is yielding dividends across both developed markets such as the UK, France and Canada, and developing markets including South Africa, India and countries in Latin America.” This sentiment resonates for Good Drinks Australia, with Chief Strategy Officer Aaron Heary explaining that the premium end of the market is where the portfolio is seeing its strongest growth. “What’s interesting from an Australian perspective is I don’t think premiumisation is just about price – it’s about what a brand offers beyond what’s in the can. Craft beer has arguably evolved to become a segment of the premium beer category. Quality is a given, but consumers are asking what a brand says about them, how it makes them feel, and whether it aligns with how they see themselves,” Heary explained. On the other hand, sitting in the premium-and-above segment, Molly Rose Brewing’s Founder and Brewer Nic Sandery has observed a decline in packaged beer sales at this end of the market in recent years. “We’re a local Melbourne brand, and the cost-of-living pressure was very clear to us and to many of our peers well before it became a major media and political talking point,” says Sandery. “Craft and independent beer has probably never had more awareness, which is a great thing – we want as many people as possible drinking interesting, diverse beer. But we’ve also seen a clear trade-down from consumers. A few years ago, someone might have bought a mixed four-pack of big, adventurous beers. Now they might buy a four-pack of pale ale or lager. Or if they were buying a four-pack from us, they might now choose a six-pack of something like Coopers instead.” As a result, Sandery says some of his peers are releasing sharper-value versions of core range pale ales and lagers to meet changing spending patterns – many of which he says are excellent. “Independent breweries do need to be realistic about where customers are at, but this doesn’t mean we lose what it means to be an independent brewery in Australia, and the reasons why we love making and serving beer. Necessity is the mother of invention, and there could be a similar beer for us in the future. “For Molly Rose, I don’t think [the lower-volume, higher-value era] changes the core of how we make beer. We already buy the best malt in the world (Voyager) and the best hops we can source from around the world. We’ve never really baulked at using as much or as little as we need to make the beer we want to make.” With non-alcoholic beer volumes growing at eight per cent in 2025, in contrast to a one per cent decline for the overall beer category, IWSR data reveals a significant opportunity for this market segment. True to the sentiment above, 29 per cent of non-alcoholic beer volumes in 2025 were in the premium-plus price bands – a 20 per cent increase from 2019. As the brewer for both Molly Rose and dedicated non-alcoholic brewery Hiatus Beers, Sandery notes a significant uptick in demand, coming from a really diverse mix of consumers. “The most exciting thing about non-alc beer is that it cuts across demographics, ages, genders, drinking occasions and reasons for drinking. In some ways, the diversity of people drinking good non-alc beer feels broader than what we traditionally saw in craft beer. “At Molly Rose, we’ve also noticed people drinking fewer beers above six per cent, and a lot of venues are less willing to take higher-ABV beers because they simply don’t move as quickly,” Sandery told Beer & Brewer. “We’ve always leaned into sessionable beers, and I take a lot of pride in building flavour and balance into lower-alcohol beers – whether that’s a radler, an English mild or a session pale ale. That might also have something to do with becoming a father six years ago and being much less interested in hangovers,” he added. While global trends indicate that innovation in the no- and low-alcohol space continues to drive sales within the beer category, Heary also attributes much of this segment’s success to its increasing relevance and alignment with the modern Australian lifestyle. “Australia has always had a strong mid-strength market, and I think that’s context you need to understand the no- and low-alcohol conversation at the moment. Drink-driving laws, high excise rates and a warm climate have historically pushed Australian drinkers toward moderation, which means a well-established mid-strength segment was already doing a lot of the work that zero-alcohol is doing in some other markets.” Good Drinks operates in the alcohol-free space with Gage Roads Yeah Buoy Non Alcoholic XPA, and while it is growing well, Heary sees a bigger opportunity to disrupt the mid-strength category. With the launch of mid-strength lager Huey, Good Drinks hopes to premiumise a segment that has historically been overlooked. “Mid-strength makes up roughly a quarter of the Australian beer market, but it’s been a category that has seen very little innovation,” he stated. “That’s why we launched Huey. It’s a full-flavoured, naturally brewed mid-strength lager in a clear bottle, and there’s nothing else quite like it in the category. When you look at the size of the mid-strength segment and how little it has been challenged, that’s where I think the real volume opportunity lies – not just for us but for the industry.” Data from the IWSR also suggests that investment in NPD across flavoured beer variants is helping to recruit drinkers from the RTD category. According to IWSR’s Radius innovation tracker, consumers are seeking fruit-forward flavours such as orchard fruits, peach, apple and pear, while cherry and berry variants are building cross-category momentum, appearing not only in beer, but also in spirits and RTDs. With countless variations of flavoured and fruit-forward drinks in Good Drinks’ portfolio, Heary has watched the Matso’s portfolio pull in customers who wouldn’t typically reach for a traditional beer, while lime lager Miller Chill helps to bridge the gap between beer and other refreshing beverage categories. However, he questions whether or not these beverages are recruiting new drinkers to the traditional beer category. “We see it particularly with female drinkers moving fluidly between categories, but they’re not necessarily bringing people to beer as a category, they are meeting consumers where they are. People move between categories depending on occasion and mood, and having a flavour-forward offer in your portfolio means you’re part of more of those occasions. “While not a beer, the Matso’s Crush range sits adjacent and is a good example of that thinking applied to RTD. We use real fruit rather than the synthetic flavours and sugars that dominate the category, and the brand has that genuine provenance story. When the consumer is already premium-minded, that stuff matters,” Heary continued. In terms of flavoured beers, Urban Alley’s Head Brewer Dove Bailey sees growth in different areas. And with many hops released these days chasing the tropical salad flavours of Citra, he sees a trend towards fruit flavour from that perspective. “Off-premise and flavoured beers seem to be linked, while the on-premise pub experience and more classic beers are linked,” he explains. From his perspective, the opportunity here is encouraging consumers to drink better lager. “West Coast Pilsner has been a great style for combining fruit-forward hops with the high drinkability of lager. Obviously Australia perfected the XPA with this very goal in mind before West Coast Pils was all the rage, but well-made hop-forward lager is a style I’d like to see better represented. “As for fruit flavoured beer, I think that is where the challenge lies. It’s often more related to trend chasing. The danger there is being caught out with a bunch of stock on hand when the trend is already over,” Bailey concluded.

The Free, Organic Myth: What Beverage Marketers Keep Getting Wrong About Acquisition Costs

Análisis crítico sobre la falsa creencia en la industria de bebidas de que el tráfico orgánico (SEO, redes sociales, contenido comunitario) es gratuito. Los marketers subestiman costos reales de producción, gestión y recursos humanos al no considerar inversión en media paid. Este enfoque afecta directamente la eficiencia de adquisición de clientes y presupuestos de lanzamiento DTC, especialmente relevante para marcas argentinas que expanden digitalmente sin contabilizar el verdadero costo operativo de estrategias orgánicas.

Texto completo del artículo
There is a belief embedded in beverage marketing culture that organic traffic is essentially free. It shows up in brand budget presentations, distributor pitches and direct-to-consumer (DTC) launch strategy decks. The logic seems airtight: search engine optimization (SEO), social media, Reddit threads and community-driven content cost nothing to place, ergo they cost nothing to run. But “no media spend” does not mean “no cost.” In practice, it often means trading dollars for slower, manual execution. For beverage brands competing in an increasingly crowded shelf and digital landscape, this framing is not just incomplete, it is actively misleading, and it leads brands to systematically undercount one of their most significant operating expenses: time. The case for organic traffic is compelling on its face. Organic search accounts for more than half of all website traffic globally, and for beverage brands driving DTC sales or on-premise discovery, the top-ranking result on Google earns a click-through rate of roughly 27.6%. These numbers make organic channels look like a bargain. What they do not show is the runway required to get there. Most beverage brand websites take three to six months to see measurable organic results, and competitive categories — craft beer, ready-to-drinks (RTDs), functional beverages, spirits — often require six to 12 months before rankings translate into meaningful revenue. The pages ranking first on Google today are, on average, nearly three years old. That is not a channel. That is a long-term infrastructure investment disguised as a marketing tactic. For most beverage companies, especially those in growth mode or launching new SKUs, the people doing the SEO work are not interns. They are content strategists, copywriters, SEO specialists and brand managers. Their time carries a real cost — often the most expensive resource in the business. When that cost goes untracked because there is no invoice attached to it, brands make decisions based on a false cost structure, overvaluing “free” channels while ignoring the operational drag of manual execution. The counterpoint to organic is usually paid advertising, and it carries its own stigma in the beverage industry. Paid channels are seen as expensive, unsustainable and blunt —especially for smaller craft producers or emerging brands with lean marketing budgets. These criticisms are fair when applied to poorly constructed campaigns. They are not fair as a general verdict. Paid search delivers results immediately. Unlike SEO, which requires months of compounding effort before producing pipeline, a well-built paid campaign can generate qualified traffic on day one — critical for seasonal launches, limited releases or regional expansion pushes. According to WordStream’s 2025 benchmark data, the average cost per lead across Google Ads is $70.11, but this number varies enormously based on campaign execution. In practice, execution is the variable — not the channel itself. The difference between a well-structured paid campaign and a poorly structured one is not marginal. It is often the difference between a campaign that drives trial and one that drains budget without return. Execution is everything. Targeting, bid strategy, creative, landing page alignment and audience segmentation each affect cost-per-acquisition significantly. But just as important is how quickly those variables are adjusted. Beverage brands that treat paid advertising as a set-it-and-forget-it channel will overpay. Those that actively manage and optimize their campaigns can reduce their cost-per-acquisition substantially, often by more than 70% compared to unoptimized baselines. One of the most overlooked sources of wasted paid spend in beverage marketing is audience mismanagement. When brands run acquisition campaigns without excluding audiences that will never convert — such as existing subscribers, loyalty members, wholesale partners and investors — they are spending real money to advertise to people who already are inside their ecosystem. This is not a targeting edge case. It is a structural inefficiency that erodes campaign performance quietly over time. The fix is straightforward but requires intentional CRM integration. By building exclusion lists from live CRM data and syncing them to ad platforms, beverage marketers can ensure that acquisition budget is deployed toward actual new prospects. When CRM, ad platforms and analytics are connected, this becomes a continuous, automated safeguard — not a one-time fix. This practice also protects brand experience. Showing a loyal customer an acquisition-stage ad for a product they already purchase creates friction and erodes the brand trust that beverage companies work hard to build. Precision in audience management is not a technical nicety. It is one of the highest-leverage levers available to any beverage brand’s paid media program, and it is widely underutilized. The organic versus paid debate is a false binary for beverage marketers. Both channels have legitimate roles in a well-constructed marketing program — whether the goal is building brand awareness in a new market, driving DTC eCommerce sales, or supporting on-premise velocity. The problem is not which channel a brand chooses. The problem is evaluating those channels with incomplete cost inputs. Organic strategies should be assessed against their true fully loaded cost, which includes the labor hours, tool subscriptions, content production and link-building investment required to generate results. Paid strategies should be assessed against the quality of their execution, not the gross spend figure alone. A useful reframe: organic is a long-term asset play that builds compounding brand equity over years. Paid is a precision tool that delivers measurable results in real time when operated correctly. Neither is free. Neither is inherently wasteful. Both reward the beverage brands that take them seriously enough to run them well. Beverage marketers who want to make better allocation decisions should start by auditing how their organization accounts for the cost of organic channel management. If time is not being tracked, the ROI comparison to paid channels is not valid. From there, any paid program should be evaluated on the quality of its audience targeting, its exclusion logic and the degree to which campaigns are being actively managed versus passively monitored. The goal is not more activity, but better, faster and more connected execution. The beverage brands generating the best results from search in 2026 are not choosing between organic and paid. They are treating them as complementary: using paid channels for immediate, measurable acquisition — new market entries, seasonal pushes, SKU launches — while building organic presence as a long-term compounding asset.
