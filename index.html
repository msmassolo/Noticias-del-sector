El movimiento refleja confianza en la demanda sostenida de bebidas funcionales/saludables y optimización operativa en uno de los mayores productores globales.

Anheuser-Busch anunció este lunes, 15 de junio, una inversión de más de 20 millones de dólares en sus instalaciones de St. Louis y Arnold, en el estado de Misuri, con el objetivo de ampliar la producción de Michelob ULTRA, renovar equipos de fábrica y envasado y abrir un nuevo centro de formación técnica para empleados del área industrial. La compañía explicó que el dinero se dirigirá a la cervecería de St. Louis y a la planta de fabricación de latas de Arnold. Según la empresa, la mejora de equipos permitirá aumentar la capacidad para marcas con fuerte demanda, entre ellas Michelob ULTRA y Busch Light. En su comunicado, Anheuser-Busch afirma que Michelob ULTRA es la cerveza más vendida del país y la que más avanza en ventas, mientras que Busch Light ocupa el primer puesto en el mercado de Misuri. La inversión forma parte del programa Brewing Futures, con el que Anheuser-Busch prevé destinar 600 millones de dólares a sus operaciones en Estados Unidos entre 2025 y 2026. La empresa vincula ese plan a la modernización de fábricas, al mantenimiento del empleo industrial y a la formación de nuevos perfiles técnicos para sus centros productivos. El anuncio incluye también la apertura de un nuevo centro de capacitación dentro de la cervecería de St. Louis. Será una extensión del Technical Excellence Center que la compañía ya opera en esa ciudad y se integrará en una red de 15 nuevos centros similares en todo el país. La firma señala que estas instalaciones servirán para formar a trabajadores en áreas mecánicas, eléctricas, digitales y operativas. Anheuser-Busch añade que su meta es formar a más del 90% de su plantilla industrial durante los próximos cinco años. La empresa asegura además que desde 2022 ya han pasado por su centro técnico de St. Louis más de 2.600 empleados. El consejero delegado de Anheuser-Busch, Brendan Whitworth, afirmó en el comunicado que St. Louis y el estado de Misuri han sido la sede histórica del grupo durante más de 165 años y que esta nueva partida busca reforzar tanto las instalaciones como el empleo local. La compañía sostiene que estas inversiones permitirán mantener a la cervecería de St. Louis como una pieza central dentro de su estructura industrial en Estados Unidos. El gobernador de Misuri, Mike Kehoe, valoró el anuncio como una nueva muestra del peso histórico del sector cervecero en el estado y del papel económico que mantiene Anheuser-Busch en la región de St. Louis. En sus declaraciones difundidas por la empresa, Kehoe vinculó esta decisión con el entorno favorable para fabricantes y cerveceras en Misuri. Junto a las mejoras industriales y formativas, Anheuser-Busch informó de que trasladará a su campus principal de St. Louis el cartel animado “A & Eagle”, procedente de su antigua instalación de Newark. La empresa indicó que ese elemento quedará instalado de forma permanente como símbolo de su vínculo con la fabricación local y con la ciudad. Para el sector de bebidas, esta operación apunta a dos líneas con efecto potencial más amplio. Por un lado, confirma que los grandes grupos siguen destinando capital a marcas con alto volumen y rotación rápida dentro del mercado cervecero estadounidense. Por otro, refuerza una tendencia cada vez más visible: la inversión no se limita a depósitos, líneas y envases, sino que incluye formación técnica para asegurar mano de obra cualificada en plantas cada vez más automatizadas. Ese movimiento puede tener interés más allá de Anheuser-Busch. La ampliación de capacidad para Michelob ULTRA sitúa el foco en las categorías que están tirando del negocio dentro de la cerveza industrial, mientras que la mejora del envasado en latas vuelve a poner atención sobre un punto básico para los grandes productores: ganar eficiencia sin frenar suministro ni distribución. Anheuser-Busch se presenta como uno de los mayores fabricantes del país y recuerda que también opera en espirituosos listos para beber, bebidas energéticas y cerveza sin alcohol. Entre sus marcas figuran Budweiser, Bud Light, Stella Artois, Cutwater Spirits, NÜTRL Vodka Seltzer y Michelob ULTRA Zero. En el mismo comunicado, la empresa señala que este año ingresó en el Missouri Manufacturers Hall of Fame. La información difundida por la compañía no precisa cuántos puestos nuevos generará esta inversión ni cuándo entrarán en funcionamiento todas las mejoras previstas en Misuri. Sí indica que el plan se ejecutará dentro del marco inversor fijado para 2025 y 2026 y que busca reforzar tanto la producción como la preparación técnica de su plantilla industrial. New research has revealed that moderation trends are changing the way people perceive no and low alcohol drinks. The data has shown that many who indulge in drinking alcohol are also seeking out non-alcoholic options as well. db reports. Findings gathered by KAM Insight and US non-alcoholic brewer Athletic shows that 94% of alcohol-free beer drinkers also consume alcohol and only 27% of social drinkers now stick exclusively to alcoholic beer on a night out. The research, which reveals that the well-known zebra striping trend – where consumers alternate between alcohol-free and alcoholic drinks – is now showing up in the data and is highly present in the UK, with the research and analysis having now uncovered that nearly half (48%) of Brits are already doing it when they visit the pub. Bacardi has lost the latest round of its long-running battle over ownership of the Havana Club rum name and its use in the US. A US Circuit Court of Appeals in Virginia has upheld the decision made by the US Patent and ‌Trademark Office (USPTO) to renew Cuba-owned Cubaexport’s federal “Havana Club” trademark in 2016 despite Bacardi’s objections. Bacardi said it was “disappointed that the court granted the Cuban government a 10-year grace period for renewing a trademark that was stolen in ​the first place.” The wrangle harks back to the early days of the Castro regime in Cuba in the 1960s when it confiscated the brand and the name from Jose Arechabala S.A, the founding company. The Arechabala family moved their production to Puerto Rico and eventually sold the Havana Club marque in 1995 to Bacardi, who have distributed the rum in the US since then. Meanwhile, a new state company, Cubaexport, continued production in Havana and appointed Pernod Ricard to distribute their Havana Club rum globally. That excluded the US because of the embargo on Cuban goods. However, in 1976 Cubaexport had successfully registered the brand name in the US, despite not being able to sell there. This, it claims prevents Bacardi from using the Havana Club name, especially as the rum is not produced in Cuba. Cubaexport tried to renew its US ‌trademark in ⁠2005, but was prevented from doing so after the US Treasury Department’s Office of Foreign Assets Control (OFAC) refused to issue it a licence. OFAC changed course and issued the licence near the end of the Obama administration, and the USPTO renewed Cubaexport’s trademark in 2016. Following this, Bacardi sued the USPTO in a Virginia federal court in 2021, ​arguing that Cubaexport’s trademark ​should have expired in 2006. ⁠US District Judge Liam O’Grady dismissed the case in 2022, finding that Bacardi could only challenge the mark through trademark office procedures. The 4th US Circuit Court of ​Appeals revived the case in 2024. US District Judge Leonie Brinkema dismissed the lawsuit again last ​year and now a three-judge ⁠ panel has his upheld his ruling. “The OFAC licence cleared the fog, removing the legal obstacle that had prevented the 2005 transfer from counting as payment,” the appeals court said. “What looked incomplete in 2006 was, by 2016, ⁠timely and ​effective.” Bacardi claims, however, that Cubaexport’s trademark will expire in a few days’ time and ​cannot be renewed under a 2024 law that bars US courts and agencies from recognising ⁠trademarks confiscated by the Cuban government. The wrangle is set to continue as both sides fight for a slice of the key US market for rum. Un tribunal de apelación de Estados Unidos falló el martes en contra de Bacardi en la larga disputa de la empresa con el Gobierno cubano sobre los derechos de marca registrada del ron "Havana Club" en Estados Unidos. El Tribunal de Apelación del Cuarto Circuito de Estados Unidos, de Richmond, rechazó el recurso presentado por Bacardi contra la decisión de la Oficina de Patentes y Marcas de Estados Unidos (USPTO) de renovar en 2016 la marca federal "Havana Club", propiedad de la empresa cubana Cubaexport, a pesar de las objeciones de la empresa. Protein bar brand Aloha made a significant shift in Walmart by moving its protein bars out of the sports nutrition aisle and into the center store snack aisle across roughly 2,000 doors in late April – a change the brand says is already driving meaningful gains in velocity. The transition into center store reflects both evolving consumer behaviors and a strategic push by Aloha to align its placement with where shoppers naturally browse for snacks, according to the company. While the brand has long been merchandised in center store at other retailers, Walmart’s scale makes the transition particularly impactful where it serves <a href="https://www.walmartconnect.com/about" target="_blank" rel="">150 million customers</a> every week online and in store. Last year, the company <a href="https://www.foodnavigator-usa.com/Article/2025/01/24/aloha-relaunches-its-plant-based-protein-bars-in-target/" target="_blank" rel="" title="https://www.foodnavigator-usa.com/Article/2025/01/24/aloha-relaunches-its-plant-based-protein-bars-in-target/">relaunched in Target</a> for the first time in seven years after the retailer dropped the brand due to low performance. Aloha’s near triple-digit revenue growth year over year expanded its manufacturing and distribution, re-opening the door to Target. In its conversations with Walmart, Aloha emphasized the opportunity to maximize velocity in center store “where consumers are expected to find us,” said Shawn McLaughlin, chief sales officer at Aloha. While protein bars have historically been split between sports nutrition and center store, the latter now accounts for the bulk of the category. “Only about 30% of the share sits over in sports and nutrition – the other 70% is in center store,” McLaughlin said. The move comes as protein bars are increasingly used beyond traditional workout occasion and toward everyday consumption, according to Julia Shapiro, Aloha’s VP of marketing. “The overwhelming majority of them are using them for multiple use cases now,” she said, adding that “snacking in between meals is… the number one use case.” That evolution has also broadened Aloha’s core audience. “Our fastest growing segment is actually Millennial women,” Shapiro said, noting rising interest in protein consumption alongside adjacent concerns like fiber and gut health. The repositioning also aligns more closely with the brand’s messaging. “We’ve always really led with real food as our… stake in the ground,” Shapiro said. “I actually think it’s just a better fit now.” The move into center store required operational and merchandising adjustments where competing in a more saturated aisle means rethinking velocity expectations and shelf strategy, McLaughlin said. “We needed to change the forecast, our expectations around velocity,” he said, noting that performance has improved significantly (between 30% to 50% increased velocity) since the transition. At the same time, “you do have to compete for merchandising space with a lot more brands,” he added. That means creating a planogram that reflects the right shelf presence and investing in visibility tactics in other areas to drive trial. According to McLaughlin more than 95% of Aloha’s bars are sold out of the planogram while the other “5% is us trying to be really creative” using end caps to bring in new consumers. The Walmart reset is also serving as a blueprint for other retailers, according to McLaughlin. The brand is currently in discussions with some Northeast accounts that still merchandise the brand in sports nutrition, using a similar argument around consumer expectations, category traffic and performance data. The shift also aligns with how retailers like Walmart and <a href="https://www.foodnavigator-usa.com/Article/2026/05/21/target-boosts-sales-with-protein-functional-beverages-in-q1/" target="_blank" rel="">Target</a> are approaching better-for-you grocery business. According to McLaughlin, Walmart’s buyer emphasized that “‘this better-for-you thing is not a trend, it’s a lifestyle’” – reflecting Walmart’s <a href="https://corporate.walmart.com/news/2025/07/30/choosing-the-wellness-brands-our-customers-love" target="_blank" rel="">initiatives to expand healthy food options</a>, allowing brands like Aloha to compete in center store and across omnichannel. Beyond merchandising, Aloha is also leaning into evolving consumer priorities to stand out in an increasingly crowded snack bar category. Value, in particular, has taken on new meaning. “People are just being more mindful and purposeful of choosing food that provides multiple benefits,” Shapiro said, adding that taste and functional benefits like protein and fiber are crucial value propositions for consumers. Fiber, she added, is quickly becoming “the second most considered nutritional fact” for shoppers. Even as the brand sharpens its positioning, Shapiro emphasized focus over broad appeal. “We try to be mindful of not being everything to everybody,” she said, pointing to Aloha’s emphasis on consistency between marketing and product experience as a driver of its “really high repeat rates.”
