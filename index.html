Arca Continental, embotelladora de Coca-Cola, alista cambios en planta de Pucusana: los detalles

Arca Continental, principal embotelladora de Coca-Cola en Perú, implementará modificaciones operativas en su planta Pucusana de Lima, enfocándose en optimizar su infraestructura logística y el taller de mantenimiento de equipos. Este movimiento refleja la estrategia de la compañía de mejorar eficiencia operativa en uno de sus complejos industriales clave en la región andina. Los cambios son relevantes para la industria por cuanto Arca Continental es actor dominante en distribución de bebidas en Perú, compitiendo directamente con embotelladores regionales y afectando capacidad productiva del portafolio Coca-Cola.

Leer nota original
Texto completo del artículo
Arca Continental, embotelladora de marcas como Coca-Cola, Inca Kola, Fanta, Sprite y San Luis en Perú, prepara nuevos cambios en su Planta Pucusana, uno de sus principales complejos industriales en Lima. La compañía busca poner mayor foco su operación logística ¿Qué cambios alista? A través del Informe Técnico Sustentatorio (ITS), la compañía informó sobre el proyecto “Modificaciones al taller de mantenimiento de montacargas en Planta Pucusana”, a ejecutarse dentro de un área de 771.26 metros cuadrados (m²) en el complejo industrial ubicado en el kilómetro 59.7 de la Panamericana Sur. La propuesta contempla nuevos ambientes operativos y administrativos, sin ampliar la superficie ocupada ni incrementar la capacidad de producción. El proyecto de Arca Continental contempla la modificación del taller de mantenimiento de montacargas ubicado dentro de su Planta Pucusana, en Lima, como parte de una estrategia para reforzar la continuidad operativa y eficiencia logística de este complejo industrial. La iniciativa se desarrollará en un área existente de 771.26 metros cuadrados (m²), sin ampliar la superficie ocupada ni incrementar la capacidad de producción de la planta. En detalle, la compañía plantea una reorganización integral del taller mediante la implementación de nuevos ambientes y una mejor distribución funcional de los espacios. Entre los componentes previstos figuran áreas de ingreso y salida de vehículos, taller mecánico, zona de lavado, área operativa, almacén de repuestos, maestranza, oficina de jefatura, sala de reuniones, área eléctrica, compresora de aire, zona de almacenamiento de aceites y una sala de capacitación ubicada en un mezzanine. La empresa explicó que el proyecto busca asegurar la operación de una flota de 80 montacargas, los cuales registran un uso promedio de 350 horas mensuales y demandan constantes trabajos de mantenimiento preventivo y correctivo. Bajo ese contexto, la nueva configuración del taller apunta a mejorar la capacidad y eficiencia de atención de los equipos, optimizando los espacios de trabajo y el flujo operativo dentro de la planta. De acuerdo al plan de Produce, las etapas del proyecto de Arca Continental contemplan una fase inicial de planificación con habilitación de oficinas y almacenes temporales, levantamiento topográfico y adecuación del terreno, seguida de una etapa de construcción con trabajos de demolición, desmontaje, obras civiles, estructuras metálicas e instalaciones eléctricas. Según el ITS, el taller continuará operando mientras se desarrollen las modificaciones. Asimismo, la inversión estimada para el proyecto asciende a S/2.049 millones, incluido IGV, y se ejecutará dentro de un área existente de 771.26 metros cuadrados (m²), sin ampliar la superficie ocupada ni incrementar la capacidad de producción de la Planta Pucusana. El ITS también señala que la vida útil del proyecto se extenderá hasta el cierre de operaciones del complejo industrial. La Planta Pucusana constituye uno de los principales complejos industriales de Arca Continental en Perú. Desde estas instalaciones, la compañía produce y embotella bebidas de marcas como Coca-Cola, Inca Kola, Fanta, Sprite y San Luis, destinadas al mercado local a través de distintos formatos de presentación. Según el ITS, la planta cuenta actualmente con 12 líneas de producción para envases PET, vidrio retornable, vidrio no retornable y Bag in Box (BIB), lo que refleja la relevancia de esta operación dentro de la estructura productiva de la embotelladora en el país. Desde 2015, la planta ha acumulado múltiples proyectos de mejora y ampliación aprobados por Produce, entre ellos nuevas áreas de oficinas, ampliaciones de plantas de tratamiento de agua, sistemas de recuperación hídrica, almacenamiento de CO₂ y N₂, cisternas, techos industriales y ampliaciones en áreas de producción.

Texto completo del artículo
Svedka Vodka, owned by Sazerac, has launched a ready-to-drink (RTD) range called Vodka Water, which is packaged in fully transparent cans. The company claims the product is the world’s ‘first’ transparent canned vodka water. “Consumers are redefining what they want from drinking occasions. They want options that fit seamlessly into their lives without sacrificing flavour or fun,” said David Binder, senior brand director at Svedka. “Svedka Vodka Water is our answer to that shift. It’s Vodka Water 2.0: a new take on a classic combination that raises the bar on both taste and transparency, delivering a cleaner, more effortless way to drink this summer in the category’s first completely clear can.” Svedka’s vodka waters come in four flavours: strawberry, peach, lime, and pineapple. Each can contains the brand’s vodka, purified water and a crisp fruit flavour. They are non-carbonated, contain 90 calories and zero sugar, and sit at 4.5% ABV. The range will be rolled out nationwide in the US this month in eight-pack multipacks (US$18.99) and 350ml single cans (US$2.99). The brand’s Svedbot will also return to support the launch in a new ad spot produced by San Francisco-based AI innovation lab Silverside. Set in a sweltering rooftop party ‘pushed to the edge by extreme heat’, the Svedbot is seen cooling down with an ice-cold Vodka Water to transform the rooftop’s atmosphere. Binder added: “Svedka has always embraced what’s next, and with Svedka Vodka Water, we’re bringing that same spirit of innovation to a category ready for a clear difference. “Our latest ad brings the Svedbot world to life, with the transparent can giving consumers an immediate visual cue for exactly what they’re getting – something simple and clean in every sip.” According to new IWSR findings, the ready-to-drink category surpassed vodka in global value last year. Furthermore, spirits-based RTDs are growing at the expense of malt versions. Sazerac agreed to buy Svedka from Constellation Brands in December 2024. The acquisition was finalised a month later. Sazerac has also acquired Western Son Vodka and RTD brand Dirty Shirley in the past year, while entering strategic partnerships with Kendall Jenner’s 818 Tequila and Alix Earle-backed SipMargs .

Why Jack Daniel’s Halo MK1 is set to be ‘iconic’

Brown-Forman lanzó Halo MK1, una edición limitada de Jack Daniel's Tennessee whiskey con packaging innovador que replica la estructura de halo de seguridad de los monoplazas McLaren de Fórmula 1. La estrategia combina innovación en diseño de botella con una alianza de co-branding con McLaren, apuntando a diferenciación premium y atracción de consumidores de lifestyle deportivo de alto valor. Este lanzamiento refleja la tendencia de las grandes destilerías de crear experiencias memorables mediante colaboraciones de lujo y packaging distintivo como drivers de posicionamiento y volumen en segmentos alcohólicos premium.

Texto completo del artículo
Brown-Forman offered up a taste of its Jack Daniel’s Tennessee whiskey brand’s limited edition release Halo MK1 this week. Prior to the event, hosted in its London headquarters, Jessica Mason discovered more about the McLaren partnership and Brown-Forman’s newest JD launch. Brown-Forman has many reasons to be proud of its latest offering from Jack Daniel’s. Not just because the bottle itself is packaged in such a way as to emulate the halo safety structure used on McLaren Formula 1 race cars, but also because the product echoes McLaren’s well-known Papaya orange accents too. The new Jack Daniel’s Halo MK1 bottle, which has been fitted with custom-engineered McLaren Formula 1 packaging, is part of the brand’s partnership with the “iconic McLaren Formula 1” team and has been described as the distillery’s most premium and limited global release in its entire history. Speaking to the drinks business about the whiskey, Brown-Forman senior manager, global strategic partnerships Jeremy Scoones said: “The partnership with McLaren started in 2023 and runs until the end of next year. Initially, it was a three year contract, and then we extended it for this year and next year.” Here, Scoones hinted that the partnership has been so successful that if talks were to ensue over extending it for a few more years he could see how this would “make sense”. “We’re in year four already of our partnership with McLaren. It’s gone very, very quickly. We’re already discussing about potentially renewing,” he added. Why is a whiskey maker from Tennessee partnering with a Formula One team? Scoones explained that “over the last few years, if you follow Formula One, the popularity has exploded, so we’re seeing sold-out races, sold-out VIP hospitality, tickets are hot, there’s been a huge amount of coverage on social media, and what’s driving all of that are several things”. He pointed out that “Formula One has a new owner, Liberty Media, which is an American company, and they’ve really opened the sport up, especially on social media, whereas before it was quite a closed sport. There’s now Drive to Survive on Netflix and there are a lot of new fans that have come to the sport through that, and then now Hollywood’s getting involved with an F1 movie with Brad Pitt. There are a huge number of new fans coming into the sport, but not just an increase in numbers and eyeballs, but also the nature of the audience is changing”. “Only a few years ago, most F1 fans would have looked like white, middle-aged and slightly balding males, and that’s changing,” Scoones highlighted and revealed that now “over half of the new Formula One fans are female, and over a quarter of them are aged between 21 and 32 so a much younger demographic coming in. This is really interesting for us”. When asked, why McLaren? Scoones also said: “Well, we really honestly couldn’t have partnered with another team, there’s no other team with which we have a really authentic connection with, and that really comes down to our founders, who were both bold, independent spirits. So, on our side, obviously, Jack Daniel, and on McLaren side, Bruce McLaren, both of them were pioneering, they weren’t afraid to take risks and they really pushed the boundaries and innovated – that provides a really nice authentic base.” He added: “The main reasons that we’re doing the partnership: one is about presence and prominence at the point of sale, which obviously these wonderful looking bottles really help us do, especially with the papaya colouration, and it’s also about meaningful difference, so making sure that Jack is part of popular culture, and this growth in fandom really enables us to do that.” According to Scoones, for the Halo MK1, “around 12,600 bottles have been made”. Not that this has been an agreement that has not been a high value tie-up. When asked how much has been invested in Brown-Forman’s Jack Daniel’s partnership with McLaren, Scoones admitted that, as an approximation, that “it’s several million a year”. The Halo MK1’s design mirrors the “halo” shape of a McLaren Formula 1 race car and the whiskey’s bottle features hand-finished cork and custom medallions. Added to this, the packaging includes the same metal, micro-suede and carbon fibre print that McLaren uses for its Formula 1 cars. As well as going out globally, Brown-Forman has been eyeing travel retail as an additional platform to show it to the world. In fact, as Scoones explained: “We wanted to actually bring it to market earlier, but it’s been quite a complicated project, as you can see from the packaging” and we “wanted the branding to be exactly on point, but it will be in GTR later on this year”. Scoones told db : “While on a standard LTO [limited time offer], the liquid is the same every year, but this one is different from a standard No.7 and has a higher ABV [the Halo MK1 is 58.7% ABV] and this is a completely new liquid that’s never been released by Jack before”. Jack Daniel’s brand ambassador Cam Dawson said: “The reason that we picked 58.7% ABV is because Bruce McLaren’s first racing number was 58 and that was in an Austin 7 and so we are giving a little nod with that point seven there.” Scoones added that it was “liquid that we had in the distillery, and we’d been sitting on it for a few years waiting for a special way to use it”. Por un lado, el vino italiano vende menos en valor en el mercado estadounidense. La caída se produce en un momento marcado por los aranceles impulsados por Donald Trump, aunque parte de esas medidas hayan sido declaradas ilegales y siga sin aclararse del todo el sistema de posibles devoluciones. The changing fortunes of alcohol in a K-shaped economy

La industria de bebidas alcohólicas enfrenta una bifurcación de mercado acelerada por inflación y cambios de comportamiento de consumo. Mientras persiste la tendencia global de 'beber menos, pero mejor' —con consumidores priorizando calidad y ocasión sobre volumen—, la presión inflacionaria está creando una economía en forma de K: segmentos premium resilientes versus creciente presión en productos de valor. Esta dinámica obliga a reposicionar portafolios y estrategias de distribución, separando canales premium de volumen, y requiere redefinir propuestas de valor en el segmento de precios accesibles para retener consumidores sensibles al costo.

Texto completo del artículo
Alcohol has already been undergoing a fundamental shift: with the long-term trend of ‘drinking less, but drinking better’. Consumers are cutting back on alcohol in an effort to be healthier. And yet, the majority are not giving up alcohol entirely: they’re just becoming more focused on what they’re drinking, when, and why. But money is now becoming an increasingly important factor. As inflation bites, consumers are tightening their purse-strings. That can mean two things for alcohol. Consumers either see alcohol as an affordable luxury: a treat that remains within reach and justified in their daily lives (unlike, for example, a new car or overseas holiday). It’s an easy and familiar indulgence in times of stress or uncertainty. But for others, it’s the very definition of a discretionary purchase. It’s a non-essential ‘want’ rather than a ‘need’ - and therefore the first to be thrown out of the shopping basket. To understand how consumers react to these new dynamics, we need to understand the K-shaped economy. A K-shaped economy is, essentially, a tale of two shoppers. Higher-income households rise along the upward arm of the ‘K’; while lower-income households fall or stagnate along the lower arm. That suggests two different and distinct groups of consumers: higher-income households spend more on alcohol; while lower-income households spend less. But that’s quite a simplistic interpretation, says Nataly Kelly, CMO of consumers insights company Zappi. The company’s research shows that even higher earners are <a href="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2026/06/04/alcohol-occasions-are-moving-from-the-bar-to-the-home/" target="_blank" rel="" title="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2026/06/04/alcohol-occasions-are-moving-from-the-bar-to-the-home/">feeling the pinch</a>. Around 41% of regular drinkers say alcohol feels less affordable than a year ago. And - crucially - that number is nearly identical among consumers who earn more than $100k a year. “Affordability pressure isn’t just isolated to lower-income consumers,” says Kelly. But the crucial point: “The differences show up in how consumers respond,” she says. “Lower income consumers are more likely to cut back outright; while higher-income groups are more likely to optimize.” So how does that play out in practice? First and foremost is a shift from bars and restaurants to at-home drinking occasions. Today, 40% of drinkers say rising alcohol prices affect their decision to go out or not. But that doesn’t necessarily mean they’re not drinking: they’re becoming more likely to drink at home (38% of drinkers now say they’re drinking more at home instead of going out to manage the cost of alcohol). And there’s another shift: <a href="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2026/06/04/alcohol-occasions-are-moving-from-the-bar-to-the-home/" target="_blank" rel="" title="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2026/06/04/alcohol-occasions-are-moving-from-the-bar-to-the-home/">the rise of pre-drinking</a>. This practice - having a drink or two at home before going out - is an easy win: the drinks at home are substantially cheaper, thus cutting the bar or restaurant bill (without having to sacrifice the socialising or the experience). But more crucial is the idea of value: a concept that now needs to be re-examined in the economic context of 2026. At its base, ‘value’ is the simple worth of a product: and how much consumers want to pay. And that’s important for higher-income consumers as well as lower-income consumers. Zappi’s research found that a third of higher-income consumers are actively looking for promotions or discounts - around the same as the figure overall. But value can also mean experience: something that makes a brand feel genuinely and tangibly superior. In the past, the term ‘premium’ could be thrown around fairly easily with few tangible benefits. Now, consumers want to see <a href="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2026/06/07/premiumisation-brands-and-private-label-are-now-about-proven-value/" target="_blank" rel="" title="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2026/06/07/premiumisation-brands-and-private-label-are-now-about-proven-value/">very clearly what they are paying extra for.</a> So what does that mean for alcohol brands? For premium brands, the demand is still there: but it needs to be navigated carefully. Diageo, for example, has built up its portfolio on premium products and that remains its key strength. And yet it has acknowledged that these price points are becoming a struggle. In response, the company is exploring smaller formats: offering <a href="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2026/03/06/diageo-takes-premium-problems-with-lower-price-points/" target="_blank" rel="" title="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2026/03/06/diageo-takes-premium-problems-with-lower-price-points/">premium products at an affordable price points.</a> There’s also a chance for private label. Particularly in Europe, the reputation of private label brands has grown enormously over the last few years: now providing a tempting alternative to the tried-and-trusted brands. Thinking of the K-shaped economy as two groups of people who either do spend or don’t spend money, therefore, becomes very simplistic. “It’s more about different coping strategies,” says Kelly of Zappi. “Consumers across income levels are becoming more intentional about where they spend, what feels worth paying for, and how they manage discretionary purchases.” And it’s not even just about alcohol. It’s about how consumers approach goods in general. “That broader value shift is something we’re seeing across categories right now,” said Kelly. “In our broader consumer trends research, nearly half of consumers identified themselves as value-seekers, signaling this mindset is becoming structural rather than temporary.”

Lo más importante de los últimos días Ver resumen del período · 8 días · Actualizado 2026-06-12

La semana estuvo dominada por la antesala del Mundial de Fútbol 2026, con AB InBev, Coca-Cola y PepsiCo ejecutando movimientos agresivos de marketing, distribución e infraestructura para capturar el gasto estimado de USD 7.500 millones asociado al torneo. En paralelo, los resultados financieros de Brown-Forman y Rémy Cointreau confirmaron la presión sostenida sobre los espirituosos premium, con guidance cautelosa para 2027 y planes de recuperación estructural en marcha. Monster Beverage se consolidó como la gran historia de crecimiento del sector, alcanzando un nuevo máximo de 12 meses con ventas internacionales ya en el 45% de sus ingresos. En Argentina, el foco local estuvo en la propuesta gubernamental de precios más claros en supermercados y el avance de las exportaciones vitivinícolas, con Trivento apuntando al liderazgo en ese segmento.

Top 5 eventos del período

  • Brown-Forman proyecta año plano pese a superar estimados en Q4, con Jack Daniel's en caída y el tequila como lastre principal de su portafolio.
  • Monster Beverage marca nuevo máximo bursátil de 12 meses con ventas internacionales en el 45% y programa de recompra ampliado a USD 900M.
  • AB InBev, Coca-Cola y PepsiCo movilizan activos para el Mundial 2026 compitiendo por los USD 7.500M de gasto CPG estimado del torneo.
  • JAB Holding completó su salida total de Keurig Dr Pepper, cerrando una desinversión estratégica relevante en el sector de bebidas sin alcohol.
  • PepsiCo lanza distribución con camiones autónomos a escala en EE.UU., marcando un hito en automatización logística para el sector de consumo masivo.
Finanzas

Brown-Forman superó expectativas en Q4 con USD 912M (+2% YoY) pero proyecta un año plano por debilidad en tequila y presión arancelaria, mientras Rémy Cointreau lanzó su plan RC Forward con objetivo de recuperar €100M de utilidades en tres años tras una caída pronunciada de ganancias. JAB Holding completó su salida total de Keurig Dr Pepper al vender su participación remanente del 4,3%, cerrando una desinversión estratégica de largo plazo en el sector de bebidas no alcohólicas.

Marketing

El Mundial 2026 es el gran activador de la semana: AB InBev amplió su acuerdo global con FIFA posicionando Michelob Ultra como eje de bajas calorías en EE.UU., Coca-Cola lanzó una alianza con Hudson en travel retail de aeropuertos y PepsiCo estrenó 'House of Treats', una plataforma experiencial para el canal on-premise. Celsius Holdings quedó bajo presión reputacional tras la investigación del Fiscal General de Texas por prácticas de marketing dirigidas a menores, un riesgo regulatorio creciente para la categoría de energéticas.

Supply Chain

Tetra Pak actualizó su estrategia de sustentabilidad con foco en abastecimiento responsable y resiliencia hídrica, mientras Suntory Beverage & Food Europe reportó una reducción del 20% en su huella de carbono, marcando el ritmo que la regulación europea exige al packaging y la producción. En EE.UU., la Corte Suprema anuló aranceles clave de la era Trump, complicando los procesos de reembolso para importadores de bebidas y generando incertidumbre operativa en cadenas de suministro internacionales.

Ventas / Comercial

PepsiCo desplegó una flota de camiones autónomos en tres estados de EE.UU. en alianza con Gatik para distribución a supermercados y centros de distribución, apuntando a reducción de costos operativos en logística de última milla. En la región, Campari Group selló una alianza con CBC, embotellador multilatino de Pepsi, para co-distribución de Aperol y Riccadonna, mientras Coca-Cola Brasil activó 11 fábricas y 53 centros de distribución en preparación para el Mundial.
