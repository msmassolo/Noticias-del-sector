The RTD, Pre-Mixed & Hard Seltzer Masters 2026 winners

Las bebidas alcohólicas listas para consumir (RTD) crecen a doble dígito en EE.UU. (+14% en volumen en 2025) mientras el mercado de spirits general retrocede (-4%), según datos de IWSR. Esta categoría demuestra ser un foco de expansión estratégica en un contexto de contracción del segmento de bebidas alcohólicas tradicionales. El crecimiento de RTD y hard seltzers representa una oportunidad clave para empresas de bebidas argentinas que busquen diversificar portfolios y capturar cambios en preferencias del consumidor hacia productos de mayor conveniencia y premiumización.

Texto completo del artículo
While the spirits industry as a whole has been experiencing turbulence in recent years, ready-to-drink expressions are a bright spot. What goes up must come down, or so the saying goes. While much of the spirits industry is on the latter part of this trajectory, one category appears to be solidly bucking the trend: RTDs. Take the US. In 2025, total spirits sales by volume fell by 4%, but spirits-based ready-to-drink (RTD) products grew by 14%, IWSR data confirms. In the US, total beverage alcohol volumes fell by 5% in 2025. RTDs were also down by 1%, IWSR notes, but this was largely due to a 5% drop in malt-based expressions. Across 22 markets, RTDs were the only major alcohol segment in growth , up by 2% in volume. The data says it all – it’s a great time to be an RTD. When it comes to brands, though, which ones are worth seeking out? The Pre-Mixed, RTD & Hard Seltzer Masters looked to celebrate those products most deserving of engagement. Two panels of judges gathered at The Singer Tavern in London to taste this year’s entrants. Sitting on the first panel were Matt Chambers and Karen Taylor, co-founders of Whisky For Everyone . Melita Kiely, editor-in-chief of The Spirits Business , and chair of The Global Spirits Masters Competitions , led the group. The second team was chaired by David T Smith, spirits writer, consultant and co-founder of the Craft Distilling Expo. He was joined by Sarah Miller, founder of the Gin A Ding Ding blog, Sara Jane Eichler, founder of Negroni Club UK, and Ruchira Neotia, drinks consultant and founder of Ruchira’s Table. The tasting began with a flight of Vodka-Based serves, two of which left the judges extremely excited for what was to come. Securing the top Master medal were Asahi Zeitaku Shibori with a Squeeze of Peach, and Asahi Zeitaku Shibori with a Squeeze of Lemon. The judges loved the peach flavour for its authentic flavours: “Juicy ripe peach aroma, natural, smells delicious. The palate is very peachy, not too sweet, with a nice level of carbonation.” Authenticity of flavour was also what propelled the lemon variant to Master status. The judges described the winning product as “zesty, slightly sour, pithy, and like a tart, natural lemonade”. A pair of Gold medals also shone in the opening flight: MG Spirit Blueberry for its “good level of fizz, pleasant sourness and notes of blueberry, raspberry and cranberry too”, and Iceland Vodka Mix (Pilih Rasa), which had “a bright citrus aroma and flat cola notes on the palate”. Four Silver medals finished the flight. “It’s clear that fresh, realistic flavours win,” said Chambers. In the Gin-Based heat, another Master medal was added to the ranks – Harahorn Gin & Tonic. Smith said this had “loads of juniper and pine on the nose – you know it’s gin – succulent, refreshing and moreish”. Seven Gold medals were also enjoyed in the gin flight, including No.3 Gin Vesper Martini. “It’s hard to pull off a freshly made Martini; this delivers. I’d keep this stored in the freezer and it’s ready to pour and go,” said Miller. Edinburgh Gin & Tonic also won a Gold medal for its “good balance of sweetness, bitterness, and light floral notes”. More greatness appeared in the Tequila/Mezcal/Agave Spirit-Based flight. The first of two Master medals was awarded to Sierra Lime Margarita, which delivered “salty lime and zestiness” on the nose, which led to “a bright and refreshing palate that sings with agave”. Also taking home a Master medal was Lagerita, which had “notes of hops and honey” on the nose, and “lots of honey and citrus, with good length and complexity” when sipped. An equal number of Gold and Silver medals, five of each, were also awarded in this flight. Gold winners included Tequila Cazadores Canned Margarita, with “light notes of agave, sour citrus and a balanced finish”. In the Whisky-Based heat Black Jack took home a Gold medal. The judges said this delivered the expected profile of a solid whisky-and-cola serve: “Easy-drinking, not overly sweet and well-balanced.” Two medals were awarded in the following Cognac/Brandy-Based flight, a Gold for “elegant” FreshKiss Saveurs d’Été, and a Master for its stablemade FreshKiss Bouquet Fruité. The Master winner was described as “bright, superb; Cognac comes through, as does the tartness of the raspberry – so good”. Five Gold medals and one Silver were awarded in the Rum-Based round. MG Destilerías collected two of the Gold awards for MG Spirit Strawberry Mojito and MG Spirit Mojito. The strawberry-flavoured expression was liked by the judges for being “bright, fresh and minty” with a “sweet strawberry and rummy character”. Up next came a flight of Liqueur/Speciality Spirit-Based pre-mixed beverages. Two Gold medals were presented to Tatratea, one for the producer’s Original Mixed with Cola, and the second for Outlaw Mixed with Tonic. “The aroma is light, leading to herbal liqueur notes and bitterness from the tonic water – an interesting alternative to a G&T,” the judges noted. A trio of Silvers rounded off the flight, including an award for Cointreau Citrus Spritz – Grapefruit & Tangerine. A flight of Spirit Blend-Based entries resulted in a pair of Gold medals for Cabaré Ice – Limão and Cabaré Ice – Tangerina. The Tangerina flavour was “bright, fruity, jammy with floral notes – easy to drink”, the experts enthused. A round of Wine-Based Cocktails also impressed the panel, with a Silver medal for Libertine Blends’ Peach Bellini, and a Gold medal for Orang Tua’s Amer Sangria Fizz. “Attractive red-orange colour, sweet citrus and jammy strawberry, and an enjoyable, long finish,” the judges said. With two flights to go, Hard Seltzers came to the fore. Two Gold medals and one Silver were celebrated, with a Gold award given to Baie Piña Colada, said to have “loads of coconut and a hint of pineapple – like a boozy sparkling coconut water”. Gold-winning Baie Pamplemousse also delighted the judges with its “decent carbonation and zesty, citrussy” profile. A Silver medal completed the flight. In the final flight of the tasting – Low & No – the last Master medal of the day was discovered: Libertine Blends Fizzy Pear & Cardamom 0%. “Magnificent cardamom-led nose, with pear coming in, along with a little waft of rose,” commented Neotia. “Refreshing and incredibly well-balanced. Absolutely delicious.” A Gold for “sweet, almost creamy” Poshe Drinks, and a Silver for Libertine Blends Hibiscus Spritz 0% finished the initial tasting. The Master medallists were repoured for the judges, who were tasked with voting for the overall favourite pre-mixed product of the day. The Fizzy Pear & Cardamom 0% serve from Libertine Blends was strongly celebrated by the judges, who noted its unique flavour combination and well-executed finish. However, it was Cognac-based FreshKiss Bouquet Fruité that took home the coveted Taste Master 2026 title. Miller noted once again the product’s “lovely rich, fruity and beautifully fragrant nose, and absolutely delicious flavour with Cognac running through to the finish”. Reflecting on the day’s tasting, Chambers said: “I remember when we first started judging RTDs, and some were dreadful. The quality is definitely improving. There were a lot of Gold and Master medals. This says a lot about how much the category is improving. It’s exciting to see.”

Today’s consumers are already <a href="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2026/01/23/alcohol-moderation-and-understanding-alcohol-free-opportunities/" target="_blank" rel="" title="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2026/01/23/alcohol-moderation-and-understanding-alcohol-free-opportunities/">cutting back on their alcohol intake</a>. In fact, in Europe, low and no alcohol drinks are now mainstream: around <a href="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2025/04/17/the-potential-of-alcohol-free-beer-in-europe/" target="_blank" rel="" title="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2025/04/17/the-potential-of-alcohol-free-beer-in-europe/">one in every 15 beers sold is alcohol-free.</a> Football may be the last bastion for beer, booze and heavy drinking: but the 2026 World Cup is already shaping up to be very different. The 2026 World Cup is being hosted by three nations: the US, Canada and Mexico. The time difference means that, in Europe, many matches will kick off later in the evening - around 9pm or 10pm or even later. And - particularly for weekday matches - that’s not a time when people want to be drinking heavily. Football matches might once have been a boozy occasion, with the festivities continuing throughout the evening. But late kick-off times will push the celebrations later into the night, with some matches finishing in the early hours of the morning. And fewer and fewer people are keen to face a hangover in the office the next day: meaning fans may think twice about ordering another round. “Moderate drinkers are increasingly important to the World Cup occasion, particularly with later kick-off times and consumers balancing work, travel and next-day responsibilities,” said Alison Jordan, CEO at market intelligence firm The Oxford Partnership. Brands, pubs and bars should think about putting no and low alcohol options front and centre, alongside their alcoholic drinks. That might mean having at least one alcohol-free beer on draught; or putting a special selection of mocktails on the menu. They should also be ready to cater for consumers who opt for smaller measures (half pints or smaller wine glasses) or more sessionable choices in the alcohol space. “Operators can appeal to these [moderating] consumers by making no and low and moderation choices highly visible and credible rather than treating them as secondary options,” said Jordan. But it’s not about shunning alcohol: with many fans still happy to have a couple of alcoholic drinks but looking to moderate their intake with strategies such as <a href="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2024/09/18/what-is-zebra-striping-an-opportunity-for-alcohol-free-drinks/" target="_blank" rel="" title="https://www.beveragedaily.com/Article/2024/09/18/what-is-zebra-striping-an-opportunity-for-alcohol-free-drinks/">zebra striping</a> (alternating between alcoholic and non-alcoholic drinks). “Consumers still want the atmosphere, social connection and shared experience of watching football in venue, they just increasingly want flexibility in how they drink,” said Jordan. “Alcohol-free beer, lower ABV options and premium, quality adult soft drinks all become much more important in that environment.”

Alcoholic beverages can now be sold between 11am and midnight in Thailand, following on from a successful six-month trial period where restrictions were eased. Carlsberg shares breakthrough hop genome research to help future-proof beer production The brewing industry’s fight against climate change has taken a major scientific step forward, with the Carlsberg Research Laboratory (CRL) unveiling what it describes as the most detailed genetic map of hops ever produced. Published in Nature Communications , the open-access research is being shared freely with scientists, breeders and growers worldwide to help accelerate the development of more climate-resilient hop varieties while unlocking new flavour opportunities for brewers. The announcement comes as hop-growing regions across Europe and North America face mounting challenges from rising temperatures, prolonged droughts and increasingly unpredictable weather patterns that are already impacting both crop yields and hop quality. Birgitte Skadhauge, vice president and head of the Carlsberg Research Laboratory, said: “Challenges like climate change are bigger than any one company. By sharing our hop genome research in Nature Communications, we are giving scientists and breeders everywhere tools to protect crops, to innovate, and to help secure the future of beer.” The new study provides an unprecedented look into the complex genetics of hops, one of beer’s four traditional ingredients alongside water, barley and yeast. CRL scientists created a high-resolution map of all the chromosomes in a commercially important hop variety, capturing both inherited chromosome sets in detail. The breakthrough allows researchers to better understand how different genetic lineages contribute to brewing traits such as flavour, aroma, resilience and yield. Hops are particularly difficult to study genetically due to their large and repetitive genome structure, which is comparable in size to the human genome. Their unusual reproductive biology further complicates breeding efforts, as only female plants produce the cones used in brewing. According to CRL, the new genome map separates European and North American hop lineages with far greater precision than previously possible, creating a clearer path for breeders developing next-generation hop varieties. Ilka Braumann, head of hop development at the Carlsberg Research Laboratory, said: “Hops are genetically far more complex than most people realise, and that complexity has limited how quickly we can improve them. By separating the European and North American lineages in the genome, we can now see how different traits come together, giving us a much clearer path to developing better, more resilient hop varieties.” By making the genome research publicly available, CRL hopes to support faster and more precise breeding efforts across the global brewing and agricultural sectors. Founded in 1875 by brewer and philanthropist J.C. Jacobsen, the Carlsberg Research Laboratory has long played a pivotal role in brewing science and beyond. The laboratory has previously contributed to major advances in barley and yeast genome research and is widely recognised for scientific breakthroughs, including the invention of the pH scale. With the publication of its hop genome research, CRL says it has now strengthened scientific understanding of all three traditional non-water brewing ingredients: barley, yeast and hops. <b>Key takeaways:</b> Heritage is one of the food industry’s most valuable assets. It creates trust, recognition and a story that newer brands often spend years trying to build. Yet heritage can also become a liability. Consumers may respect long-established brands, but that doesn’t mean they’ll continue buying them. Many fade as tastes change, retail evolves and shoppers gravitate towards products that feel more relevant to their lives. That challenge sits at the heart of PepsiCo’s thinking around Duyvis, the Dutch nuts brand celebrating its 220th anniversary this year. While the milestone offers an opportunity to look back, the Netherlands-based PepsiCo-owned brand is far more focused on what comes next. According to Jan van Twillert, marketing VP, PepsiCo Western Europe, the reason Duyvis has remained commercially competitive for more than two centuries has little to do with nostalgia. “Duyvis’ ability to remain relevant over more than two centuries comes down to consistently balancing heritage with reinvention,” he says. At a time when brands are under pressure to respond to changing health priorities, premiumisation trends, new consumption occasions and shifting media habits, the global giant believes the challenge isn’t preserving heritage but continually finding new ways to make it matter. The objective is no longer simply to win share from competitors but to create additional reasons for consumers to engage with a category.
